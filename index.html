Déjà Vu

Photographs by Gail Albert-Halaban

Déjà vu can be powerful, jarring, nostalgic, or even transportive. Though only a moment in time, déjà vu can present us a rare feeling that time is both linear and abstract.

It’s a sensation you might feel when looking at Gail Albert Halaban’s new work. Her photographs of shops, cafes, and public spaces are immediately familiar, but there is something else happening within these images that draws the viewer into them. Assembled from dozens of frames taken at different angles and times of day, the images are an amalgam of our psyche, a collective look at spaces we might pass by hundreds of times and, whether consciously or subconsciously, record our own fractured memories of these structures.

Conversation around photography is certainly connected to the passage of time. Photographs are record keepers, markers of a specific moment that can be nostalgic, awakening and reminding us of who we were or where we were or even what we were during those moments. Although literally moments in time Albert-Halaban’s photographs morph into something more abstract, a collage of our histories and our individual and sometimes collective thoughts we connect with an image.

Gail Albert Halaban has always been interested in the ways in which we connect with one another, often serendipitously. A few years ago, she began documenting community spaces that acted as a backdrop for these interactions. Photographed at different times during a 24 hour period, Albert-Halaban’s images have a sense of loneliness: streets are quiet, some buildings look old or in disrepair, sometimes a person or two sits quietly at a table, or a solo figure fades into the background.

Regardless of time of day or whether human subjects interact with these spaces, what remains are the structures themselves, concrete and glass, static and solid, inviting us inside to find connection. And also warning us that opportunities for connecting are fragile.

Albert-Halaban was inspired to create this work after watching her spin classes shrink as members chose Peletons for their homes, noticing her local cafes quieted as people began preparing Nespressos in their kitchens, buying their avocados on Instacart, and feeling public interactions becoming more about avoidance as we walked face down staring at a screen. Mom and pop stores vanished and were replaced by big box stores – or nothing at all.

Her new work began to evoke a eulogy to what we once thought of as city living. In city life, the storefronts, coffee shops, restaurants, and residential spaces become a part of one’s daily nourishment. To watch those spaces disappear is often seen as an affront to everything each of us cultivated while living alongside these structures. As our worlds become more homogenized and unique spaces disappear, replaced and changed multiple times a year, we often aren’t able to remember what was ever there in the first place. Albert-Halaban’s work celebrates our memories of how we once felt in these spaces, sparking a warm sense of déjà vu, and causing a desire to go there again before they disappear.

— David Rosenberg
